Shade House

SH-2022.Apr.15

I use a 3D software called Blender - completely free software, mind you, download, install, PLAY! - to help visualise some ideas I had for this shadehouse:

Note on 22.Apr.25: the more I’ve been working on and thinking about the design, the more I realise that it’s probably not practical to have a gabled roof of the type in that image. Perhaps a lean-to approach might be more useful.

Started with: removal of all the gardening stuff (pots and tools and soil in bags and clay marbles for hydroponics and on and on, endless collection of stuff over the years!) that had accumulated over time - washed anything that looked useful; if not, it got thrown - and dismantled the bed furthest from the camera:

When the decking came up, I became aware of something I’d sort-of suspected for some time: there was a real problem with wood breakdown. Rot. The floor frame (under the planking) had terrible rot issues. The foundation will have to be replaced:

Growing Things

GT-2022.Apr.18

And why do all this? For the cuccumbers

and parsley

and echallots (spring onions):

When you buy echallots here in Oz, get generally get the complete plant: roots and all. I have found that if you cut off the bottom bit (leaving about 2 inches or so) you can stick these bottom bits into the ground and they will grow into full, healthy plants.

Using this approach, we used to have tons of spring onions! almost never had to buy any for quite a while. Lately, though, these stumps I planted never seemed to survive longer than a night or so. We finally figured it’s likely a rat or a possum having a midnight snack.

So, we’re going to try this approach - protecting the stumps until they get going growing - to give the plant a change at successful growth by keeping it covered.

Indeed, I’ll probably have to take this approach with any seedlings. I’d planted a whole bunch of broccoli and beetroot seedlings and the whole lot got decimated by some critter. Very frustrating!

We’ll see how we go.

We had the footpath around the house worked on a few years back. Sadly, rain and wear-and-tear (and probably also poor design) left things looking a bit worse for wear:

FP-2021.May.27

I tore out the rotten timber and sifted out the pumice-like gravel, rescuing what I could of the original river-rock:

then raked down the crusher-dust under this rock-gravel mix. This stuff was contaminated with dirt, so a perfect medium for growing weeds. I had the evidence from my front garden bed area that weeds will not grow if the river-rock is deep enough so removing this dirty crusher dust was crucial. The replacement timber was painted with two coats of Timber Protecta (to prevent rot) prior to placement:

The timber lengths are joined together thus:

and each length was bolted to a piece of treated timber to prevent sagging in the future:

The reason I thought it a good idea to attach it diagonally was in order to have a maximum of rock holding the piece down (surface-area thing). We’ll see if it works, longterm.
